IP range blocks Edit

Latest comment: 13 February8 comments5 people in discussion

Please reduce the size of your IP range blocks. A /32 block on IPv6 are 79.228.162.514.264.337.593.543.950.336 (79 decillion) different IP addresses, that's just excessive. In the case of 2a02:c7c::/32 it blocked a huge UK ISP. Please try to look for the smallest possible range when blocking. Specially in the case of IPv6, they are usually given out as /48 or smaller blocks, so any block range above that is most likely too large. If you are unsure about a block range, it might be better to just rely on single or small range block instead of trying to fit them all into one large range block. -- MarkusRost (talk) 00:50, 13 February 2024 (UTC)Reply[reply]

Well, unfortunately, that is next to impossible because of how IP ranges work. I literally tried putting in random numbers (/5 or /48 as examples) and I get either no results or an error saying that the range is larger than /32. It also doesn’t help that I can’t pinpoint to a particular range because attempting to do so will also yield back to a /32 range or no results. BDJP (t|c) 01:08, 13 February 2024 (UTC)Reply[reply]
Maybe mw:Help:Range_blocks/IPv6 will help explaining. GIM Dianliang233 T C 01:17, 13 February 2024 (UTC)Reply[reply]
Not helping, unfortunately. I put in ten random numbers after 2a02:c7c::(/nn) and again, nothing. BDJP (t|c) 01:22, 13 February 2024 (UTC)Reply[reply]
That's because you went of the first IP in the /32 block, which is so huge it contains a lot of smaller ranges but using the same base looks only at the first smaller range, ignoring all the others: 2a02:c7c::/32 = 2a02:c7c:..., 2a02:c7c::/48 = 2a02:c7c:0000:...
It's easier to go of an IP that actually has edits and add the range there. 2A02:C7C:0:0:0:0:0:0/32 is most likely multiple different editors anyway, but more acceptable ranges I could find are 2A02:C7C:A22C:9600:D000:0:0:0/68, 2A02:C7C:8901:2600:0:0:0:0/64 and 2A02:C7C:5885:7900:6000:0:0:0/67, however none of them have activity that would warrant a range block. -- MarkusRost (talk) 01:32, 13 February 2024 (UTC)Reply[reply]
If you don't fully understand how IP ranges work, you can follow the advice in the help page linked by Dianliang233 and just block the /64 range as that's about equal to blocking a single IPv4 address. -- MarkusRost (talk) 01:35, 13 February 2024 (UTC)Reply[reply]
If I can throw my 3 cents in the conversation (because v6 networking is kind of my pet peeve), blocking /64 IPv6 range is equivalent to blocking a single public IPv4, and a single public v4 can be used by multiple customers of a single ISP if said ISP uses NAT444 (aka CG-NAT). IPv6 is always public (as in: no NAT, computers and phones have their own public IPs), and you always target a network of a singe customer. Defaulting to /64 range is a good practice, because you block the smallest usable subnet that for practical purposes is almost definitely the entire network and all devices in a single customer's household.
You can go with /56 (256 individual /64s) if you want to be sure you target the entire prefix allocation of a single customer (smallest prefix residential customers gets, nearly all ISPs supporting v6 give that), but going with prefixes larger than /48 is a very, very bad idea because you leave the territory of "a single residential or business customer" and go into "IPv6 block of the entire ISP". Keep in mind IPv6 prefix sizes grow exponentially.

tl;dr in v6, default to blocking /64. In v4, be very careful with any range blocks.

Cheers, Rail (talk) 11:39, 13 February 2024 (UTC)Reply[reply]
Your technical expertise is always appreciated, Rail. --AttemptToCallNil (talk) 11:48, 13 February 2024 (UTC)Reply[reply]
