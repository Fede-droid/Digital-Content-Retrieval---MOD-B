Wanting to learn from you! Edit

Latest comment: 8 February3 comments2 people in discussion

Hey Simanelix! I've seen you're a very active member of the wiki with a pretty high edit count. I also see you're a very new member. I'm also very new but I haven't found many opportunites to edit the wiki, so I wanna ask you, how do you do it? What's your thought process when editing a page? I'd like to be more active here and I think your input would really help me. Thanks in advance! --Eatyourglory (talk) 15:47, 5 February 2024 (UTC)Reply[reply]

I've been thinking about you post all day, since I read it.
"How do you do it?" is a pretty generic question to ask. Considering that the answer is simply "I click the edit / edit source buttons and use my computer", it seems like a useless question to ask. However, it is a really good question!
How I write for articles here on the wiki is simple:
I follow my own personal style guide.
My personal style guide:
  • Redundancy - we wanna make sure to avoid repeating information, unless the information is being repeated in a:
    • a summary
    • a different medium
    • a different context
  • Readability - we wanna make sure people can actually read what we are writing:
    • no obscure words in general contexts, like "entropy" in a context where "damage" or "age" caries the same message
    • keep things short!
      • sometimes this means omitting words, like "the" and "that"
      • write "pencil box" instead of "box of pencils"
      • etc... (sorry, but I don't have any more examples)
    • use logical ordering
      • in a sequence of events, try to put them in consecutive order
        • don't say "the door opens after receiving a redstone signal"
      • if A proves B, put A first
        • example: "the warden is a mob that keeps players on their toes" instead of "in order to keep players on their toes, the warden was added"
      • if one thing contains another, put the container first
        • "the blacksmith building contains an anvil" is better than "anvils can be found in blacksmith buildings"
          • in this example, we would use the latter sentence on Anvil's page, since listing the most relevant item first takes precedence
      • list the main subject of the most logical item first
        • on Creeper's page, "creepers only automatically agrro on players" rather than "players are the only entity which automatically aggroes a creeper"
    • Good structure:
      • Prose needs to have a smooth flow. Flow is highly subjective, and is based on the way people speak in the language's vernacular.
      • Use bullets when prose has a lot of commas.
        • Exception: sometimes bullets take up too much space, so we just use prose
      • Make as many headers as possible
        • Headers have the same function as bullets. They break up long chains of paragraphs, and force us to organize our paragraphs. We don't want a paragraph that mentions a block's history, usage, and presence in world generation all at once. We want to split things up into separate sections.
          • However, headers can ruin flow, distract the eye, and take up too much space sometimes.
            • Just like with bullets, we should avoid adding new headers, as long as the prose has good flow, and as long as the different paragraphs within a section make sense together.
I hope this was not too much or anything. Also, I'm not quite done. I have a 1000 more things to say about my writing style and my mindset.
----Simanelix (T|C) 22:27, 5 February 2024 (UTC)Reply[reply]

Tables Edit

Latest comment: 24 March2 comments2 people in discussion

I would like to point out that tables typically do not scale well on mobile devices. Tables with more than two or three columns, such as are used on explorer map, tend to push relevant information offscreen completely with no indication that it exists. While you can still view the information by scrolling horizontally, it's unintuitive if you're not already well familiar with them and do not realize that information is potentially missing, and so tends to alienate mobile readers. Could you try to keep most of important information in plain text or lists, when you can? While a slightly repetitive, the format that was used before was far more mobile-friendly.
